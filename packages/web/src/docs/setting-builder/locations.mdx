# Locations and Hierarchies

Settings in ArcAgentic can describe entire worlds, but many stories play out in smaller places: cities, taverns, streets, and rooms.

This page explains how to think about locations and hierarchies so your worlds feel grounded and consistent.

---

## Why Use Location Hierarchies?

A location hierarchy answers the question: _"Where exactly is this scene happening?"_

Instead of just saying "a tavern", you can place it inside a town, region, and world. This gives the AI richer context for:

- Local culture and laws
- Climate and environment
- Who might show up or pass through

**Example hierarchy:**

```text
World: Eldoria
  └─ Region: Northern Territories
      └─ Location: Frostpeak Village
          └─ Area: The Rusty Anvil Tavern
```

---

## Thinking in Layers

When designing a setting, it helps to think in layers:

1. **World** – The broadest context (planet, realm, dimension)
2. **Region** – Large areas inside the world (kingdoms, continents, districts)
3. **Location** – Towns, cities, landmarks, dungeons
4. **Area / Room** – Specific places where scenes happen (tavern room, throne room, alley)

You do not need to build every layer for every story. Start with what you actually use.

---

## Practical Examples

### Small Story: One Tavern

If your whole session happens in a single tavern, you might only define:

- **World:** The Moonlit Road
- **Location:** The Moonlit Tavern

You can include the surrounding world in the description, but you don't need a full map.

### Bigger Story: A City and Its Districts

For a city-focused campaign, you might use:

- **World:** The City of Halebrook
- **Region:** Riverfront District, Old Quarter, Noble Heights
- **Locations:** Individual streets, markets, mansions, guild halls

This lets you move sessions between districts while keeping a strong sense of place.

---

## Using Locations in Descriptions

Even if the app does not expose every layer as separate UI fields yet, thinking in these terms helps you write better descriptions.

Try including:

- **World-level details:** technology level, magic, overall tone (grimdark, whimsical, etc.)
- **Region-level details:** climate, local customs, common dangers
- **Location-level details:** smells, sounds, typical crowds
- **Area-level details:** specific objects, lighting, points of interest

**Example (tavern area):**

> A cramped corner table near the hearth, with a clear view of both the door and the bar. The tabletop is scarred by old knife marks, and someone has carved a map of the nearby woods into the wood.

---

## Keeping Track of Places

As your world grows, you may want to:

- Use consistent names ("Moonlit Tavern" vs "The Moonlit Tavern")
- Reuse tags to group locations (e.g., `tavern`, `capital-city`, `dangerous`)
- Maintain a simple note or document listing important locations and how they connect

Future versions of ArcAgentic will integrate more detailed location structures into the runtime. For now, treating locations as layered descriptions already gives the AI a lot to work with.
