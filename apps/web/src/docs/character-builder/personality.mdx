# Personality Guide

This page explains what the **Personality** section in the Character Builder actually does in the app: which fields the AI uses today, and which ones are mainly for organizing your ideas or future features.

You do **not** need to fill in every advanced option to get good results.

---

## Two Layers of Personality

The Character Builder stores personality in two places:

1. **Quick Traits (`personality`)** - a simple comma-separated text field
2. **Structured Personality Map (`personalityMap`)** - sliders and dropdowns (Big Five, values, fears, speech style, etc.)

How they are used:

- Quick Traits are still sent to the model as a **single line of text** (e.g., "Personality traits: friendly, cautious, curious") and shown in the UI.
- The **Personality Map** is the main source of structured behavior data. Today, these parts are actively used in prompts:
  - **Big Five sliders (`dimensions`)** - converted into a short temperament summary
  - **Core Values (`values`)** - added as a line like "Core values: loyalty, curiosity"
  - **Speech Style (`speech`)** - vocabulary level, formality, directness, etc., used to steer how the character talks
- Other sections (fears, attachment style, social patterns, stress behavior) are **saved with the character and editable in the UI**, but are not yet heavily used by the runtime agents.

If you are new to the tool, you can safely focus on:

1. Quick Traits
2. Big Five sliders
3. 1-3 Core Values
4. Speech Style

Those are the fields that have the clearest effect on dialogue right now.

---

## Quick Traits (Legacy `personality` Field)

At the top of the Personality section you will see **"Quick Traits (comma-separated keywords)"**. This maps to the `personality` field in the character profile.

- Purpose: a **short human-readable summary** of how the character behaves
- Format: plain text list, e.g. `friendly, cautious, curious`
- How the app uses it today:
  - Included as a single line in the character's system prompt
  - Shown in the builder UI and used when mapping forms to the stored profile

Good Quick Traits are:

- Short and concrete: "protective of younger siblings" instead of just "nice"
- About **visible behavior**, not backstory: "tends to over-explain" instead of "was orphaned young"

> Recommendation: Use Quick Traits as a **label or elevator pitch**, not as your only source of personality detail. The sliders and values below give the model more structured guidance.

---

## Big Five Sliders (`personalityMap.dimensions`)

The **Big Five Dimensions** subsection lets you set slider values (0-1) for personality dimensions such as openness, conscientiousness, extraversion, agreeableness, and neuroticism.

These sliders are:

- Stored in `personalityMap.dimensions`
- Converted into short temperament phrases in the NPC agent
- Injected into the dialogue system prompt so the AI sees a summary like "high openness, low extraversion".

You do not need to know the psychology terms in detail. Rough guidance:

- **Openness** - prefers novelty vs. routine
- **Conscientiousness** - organized vs. improvisational
- **Extraversion** - outgoing vs. reserved
- **Agreeableness** - cooperative vs. combative
- **Neuroticism** - emotionally volatile vs. steady

Move the sliders to match how you imagine the character behaving in most situations.

---

## Core Values (`personalityMap.values`)

The **Core Values** subsection lets you pick values from a dropdown and give each a priority.

- Stored in `personalityMap.values`
- Sent to the model as a line like **"Core values: loyalty (high), justice (medium)"**
- Used to hint what the character cares about when making choices

Suggestions:

- Choose **2–4** values
- Use the **priority** field to mark which ones are driving forces for the character

Examples:

- "Loyalty to friends above all"
- "Justice, even when the law disagrees"
- "Knowledge and secrets"

---

## Speech Style (`personalityMap.speech`)

The **Speech Style** subsection controls how the character tends to talk. It includes fields like:

- Vocabulary level (simple vs. complex)
- Sentence structure (short vs. long/flowery)
- Formality (casual vs. formal)
- Humor frequency and type (none, dry, slapstick, etc.)
- Expressiveness, directness, and pace

These settings are:

- Stored in `personalityMap.speech`
- Included in the dialogue system prompt so the model is told, for example, to "use simple vocabulary, speak casually, and make occasional dry jokes".

You can treat this section as a set of **style knobs**. If you leave them at defaults, the character will still work; adjusting them makes the voice feel more distinct.

---

## Fears, Attachment, Social Patterns, Stress (`personalityMap.fears`, `attachment`, `social`, `stress`)

These subsections let you record richer psychological details:

- **Fears** - categories (e.g. abandonment, confinement), specific fears, triggers, and intensity
- **Attachment Style** - e.g. secure, anxious, avoidant
- **Social Patterns** - stranger default, how quickly they warm up, preferred role in groups, conflict style, response to criticism, boundaries
- **Stress Behavior** - primary/secondary stress responses, threshold, recovery rate, soothing activities, and stress indicators

Current behavior:

- All of this data is **saved on the character** and can be edited in the UI.
- It is **not yet deeply woven into prompts** (beyond the Big Five, values, and speech style described above).

You can still use these sections as structured notes about how you intend the character to behave. Future agent updates will be able to lean on this data more heavily.

---

## Trait Prompt IDs (`personalityMap.traits`)

The **Trait Prompt IDs** input lets you specify internal trait IDs such as `friendliness:high` or `speech:formal`.

Under the hood:

- The string you type is stored in `personalityMap.traits`.
- The builder validates combinations using `validateTraitSet` and will show **hard** and **soft conflict** warnings if you pick logically clashing traits.
- A registry of trait IDs and their prompt fragments exists in the schema, ready to be used in prompts.

As of now, these trait IDs are **validated and stored**, but not yet turned into additional prompt lines at runtime. Think of them as an **advanced, future-facing feature**.

---

## Practical Starting Recipe

For a new character, you can keep things simple:

1. Fill in **Quick Traits** with 3–5 short phrases.
2. Adjust the **Big Five sliders** to match your mental picture.
3. Add **2–3 Core Values** with priorities.
4. Tweak **Speech Style** if you want a very formal, very casual, or very humorous voice.

You can come back later to add detailed fears, attachment style, and stress patterns once you have seen the character in a few sessions.
